Introduction to Threat Modeling

What is Threat Modeling?

Threat modeling is a systematic approach to identifying, analyzing, and addressing potential security risks in systems or applications. By proactively evaluating threats, organizations can better understand their vulnerabilities and implement effective mitigations.

This process answers four key questions:

  1. What are we building? (Understand your architecture, components, and data flow.)
  1. What can go wrong? (Identify threats and vulnerabilities.)
  1. What are we doing to protect it? (Define security controls.)
  1. Did we do a good job? (Validate mitigations and reassess as needed.)

Why is Threat Modeling Important?

  • Helps prioritize risks based on likelihood and impact.
  • Reduces costly rework by addressing vulnerabilities early in development.
  • Ensures compliance with regulations (e.g., GDPR, PCI-DSS).
  • Enhances team awareness of security best practices.

Detailed Steps in Threat Modeling

  1. Understand the System
    • Create a Data Flow Diagram (DFD):

      Visualize how data moves through your system, including processes, data stores, and external interactions.

      Example: A web application DFD might include components like users, web servers, APIs, and databases.

    • Define the System Boundaries:

      Determine where your control begins and ends. For example, does your scope include third-party integrations?

  1. Identify Assets

    Assets are anything of value to the organization that could be a target for attackers. Categorize them into:

    • Data Assets: Personally identifiable information (PII), credit card details.
    • System Assets: Servers, APIs, cloud resources.
    • Operational Assets: Monitoring systems, backups.
  1. Identify Threats

    Use frameworks like STRIDE, Kill Chains, or Attack Trees to categorize potential threats.

    • Attack Trees: Break down a potential attack scenario into smaller, actionable steps.Example: To compromise a database, an attacker might:
      • Exploit weak credentials → Gain unauthorized access.
      • Perform SQL Injection → Extract sensitive data.
  1. Map Threats to Controls

    Determine the effectiveness of existing security measures and identify gaps.

    Example: For mitigating SQL Injection, ensure input validation and parameterized queries.

  1. Evaluate and Prioritize Risks

    Use models like DREAD to rank risks by:

    • Damage: How severe is the impact?
    • Reproducibility: How easy is it to replicate the attack?
    • Exploitability: How complex is the exploit?
    • Affected Users: How many users would be impacted?
    • Discoverability: How easy is it to discover the vulnerability?
  1. Define Mitigations

    Propose actionable steps to reduce risks. For instance:

    • Threat: Cross-Site Scripting (XSS).Mitigation: Implement Content Security Policies (CSP) and sanitize inputs.
  1. Validate and Iterate

    Regularly revisit the threat model to adapt to system changes, new threats, or evolving attacker techniques.

Practical Example

Taking an example of very common and generic architecture and differentiate the Assets, Controls and Threat Mapping into it.

  1. Assets:

    Assets are the core resources that an organization strives to protect. These encompass both physical assets (e.g., buildings, machinery, and hardware) and digital assets (e.g., sensitive data, software applications, intellectual property, and customer information). In the context of threat modeling, identifying assets is the crucial first step. It allows the organization to assess and prioritize security efforts based on the value and importance of each asset. The more critical the asset, the higher the priority for safeguarding it against potential threats. By understanding the asset landscape, companies can better allocate resources and develop effective protection strategies.

  1. Control:

    Controls are the security mechanisms and policies implemented to safeguard assets from various threats. These controls can be categorized into three primary types:

    • Administrative controls: These include policies, procedures, and governance frameworks (e.g., access management policies, security training, and risk assessments) that set the rules for safe operation and help ensure compliance with security standards.
    • Technical controls: These are the tools and technologies deployed to protect digital environments (e.g., firewalls, encryption, intrusion detection systems, and multi-factor authentication). Technical controls work to detect, prevent, and mitigate threats at the system and network levels.
    • Physical controls: These refer to physical barriers or mechanisms designed to protect physical assets and facilities (e.g., locks, surveillance systems, security personnel, and perimeter defenses). They are essential for protecting against unauthorized access to hardware and data storage devices.

Assessing existing controls and identifying any gaps is crucial for improving an organization’s overall security posture and ensuring that assets are adequately protected.

  1. Threats or Threat Mapping:Threats are any potential events or activities that could exploit vulnerabilities in the system, resulting in harm or damage to an organization’s assets. These threats may come from various sources, including:
    • Malicious actors: Hackers, cybercriminals, or even insiders with ill intentions can exploit weaknesses to steal, alter, or destroy data.
    • Natural disasters: Events like earthquakes, floods, fires, or severe weather conditions can impact physical infrastructure and data storage systems.
    • Accidents: Unintentional events, such as human errors or system malfunctions, can result in security breaches or data loss.
    • Technological failures: Hardware malfunctions, software bugs, or outdated systems can create vulnerabilities that cyber attackers can exploit.

Threat mapping is the process of identifying and analyzing these potential threats in the context of the assets and controls in place. It involves assessing the likelihood of each threat occurring and the potential impact it would have on the organization's operations. By mapping threats to assets, security teams can prioritize which vulnerabilities to address first and determine the most effective mitigation strategies to minimize risk.

Getting Started with Threat Modeling

To implement threat modeling:

  1. Create three tables: Assets, Controls, and Threat Mapping.
  1. Map:
    • Assets as A1, A2, … An.
    • Controls as C1, C2, … Cn.
    • Threats as T1, T2, … Tn.
  1. Use frameworks like STRIDE to identify and categorize threats.
  1. Regularly update the threat model to reflect system changes.

Table 2- Controls

No. of ControlsList of ControlsDescription
C1DNSSECDomain Name System Security Extensions (DNSSEC) add cryptographic authentication to DNS responses. This ensures the integrity and authenticity of DNS data, preventing DNS spoofing or man-in-the-middle attacks.
C2DNS Filtering and BlacklistingDNS filtering and blacklisting services help block access to malicious or unauthorized domains. By monitoring and filtering DNS traffic, organizations can prevent users from inadvertently accessing harmful websites or services that might contain malware or phishing attempts.
C3DNS Rate LimitingDNS rate limiting controls the number of requests made to DNS servers from individual clients or IP addresses. This prevents DNS amplification attacks and Denial of Service (DoS) attacks by limiting excessive or malicious query traffic that can overwhelm the servers.
C4SSL/TLS EncryptionImplement SSL/TLS encryption to secure communications between clients (such as web or mobile applications) and CDN edge servers. This ensures that sensitive data transmitted through the network is protected from eavesdropping, tampering, and man-in-the-middle attacks.
C5Origin AuthenticationOrigin server authentication involves validating the authenticity of the servers interacting with the CDN. By ensuring that only trusted servers can communicate with the CDN, organizations can prevent unauthorized access and mitigate risks like DNS hijacking and spoofing.
C6Access Control PoliciesDefine and enforce access control policies to regulate who can access cached content on CDN edge servers. Policies can be based on user roles, geographic location, device types, and other attributes to prevent unauthorized access and maintain compliance with security regulations.
C7Content Security Policies (CSP)Implement CSP headers to define and enforce security policies for web content. CSP mitigates cross-site scripting (XSS), data injection, and other attacks by restricting how resources such as scripts, images, and stylesheets are loaded and executed within web pages served through the CDN.
C8Access ControlImplement stringent physical and logical access controls within the data center. This includes securing access to critical components such as web servers, cache storage, and databases using techniques like multi-factor authentication (MFA), authorization, and enforcing the principle of least privilege.
C9Network SegmentationNetwork segmentation divides the network into smaller, isolated segments to limit the movement of attackers within the system. By isolating sensitive systems, such as databases or application servers, from less critical areas, organizations can reduce the risk of unauthorized access and contain breaches more effectively.
C10EncryptionEncrypt sensitive data both in transit (during transmission over the network) and at rest (while stored in databases or cache). This protects confidential information from unauthorized access, even in cases of network interception or physical theft. Common encryption protocols include SSL/TLS for data in transit and AES for data at rest.
C11Regular Patching and UpdatesRegularly patch and update all software, operating systems, and firmware of key infrastructure components such as web servers, cache storage, and databases. Applying updates ensures that security vulnerabilities are addressed promptly, reducing the risk of exploitation by attackers.
C12Data Backup and RecoveryRegular data backups are critical for ensuring data integrity and availability. By storing backup copies of critical data in secure locations and testing recovery procedures, organizations can ensure business continuity and minimize data loss in case of system failures or cyberattacks.
C13Monitoring and LoggingImplement continuous monitoring and logging to track network traffic, system performance, and user activities within the data center. Real-time detection of anomalies through log analysis and monitoring enables rapid identification and response to potential security incidents, improving incident response and reducing the window of exploitation.

Explanation of Controls:

  • C1, C2: DNSSEC, DNS Filtering & Blacklisting – Secures DNS traffic by preventing DNS spoofing, cache poisoning, and ensuring secure access to domains.
  • C3: DNS Rate Limiting – Helps protect against denial-of-service (DoS) attacks targeting DNS services and mitigates exposure risks.
  • C4: SSL/TLS Encryption – Ensures data integrity and security during transmission, particularly for sensitive data.
  • C5: Origin Authentication – Verifies and secures connections to origin servers, ensuring that only legitimate traffic reaches the CDN or web application.
  • C6: Access Control Policies – Restricts access to resources based on roles and permissions to minimize unauthorized access.
  • C7: Content Security Policies (CSP) – Protects against malicious content injection, such as XSS and CSRF.
  • C8: Access Control (Physical & Logical) – Limits access to data centers, servers, and databases, and enforces least privilege access to sensitive assets.
  • C9: Network Segmentation – Prevents lateral movement of threats by isolating network components, reducing the attack surface.
  • C10: Data Encryption – Ensures sensitive data is encrypted both in transit and at rest to prevent unauthorized access or leaks.
  • C11: Regular Patching, Monitoring, & Logging – Ensures systems are kept up to date with security patches, and activities are logged and monitored for signs of abnormal behavior.
  • C12: Data Backup & Recovery – Regularly backs up critical data to ensure its availability and integrity in case of data loss or system failure.
