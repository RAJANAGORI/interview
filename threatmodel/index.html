Introduction to Threat Modeling

Threat modeling is a process used by cybersecurity professionals to identify, understand, and mitigate potential security threats. It involves defining system requirements, identifying potential threats to the system, rating the threats based on potential impact and likelihood of occurrence, and finally, defining countermeasures to prevent, or mitigate the impact of, the threats. This proactive approach to identifying vulnerabilities can help organizations protect their systems and data more effectively.

There are the majorly 3 part that plays a vital role in Threat Modeling

  1. Assets
  1. Control
  1. Threat Mapping

Before jumping into the actual process, Most of the company are following the STRIDE`A methodology to perform the Threat Modeling of the giving architecture.

STRIDE

S(Spoofing)

  • A threat where one person or program successfully masquerades as another by falsifying data, thereby gaining illegitimate access.

T(Tampering)

  • Tampering involves the unauthorized modification or alteration of data or systems, leading to potential security breaches or malfunctions.

R(Repudiation)

  • This threat concerns the inability to verify the identity of users or the origin of a particular action or piece of data, which can lead to disputes or denials.

I(Information Disclosure)

  • Information disclosure threats involve the exposure of sensitive information to unauthorized parties. This can occur through various means such as hacking, data leaks, or inadequate access controls.

D(Denial of Service)

  • Denial of Service attacks aim to disrupt the availability of services, systems, or networks, rendering them inaccessible or unusable for legitimate users.

E(Elevation of Privileges)

  • Elevation of Privilege threats involve the unauthorized escalation of user privileges, granting individuals or programs access to resources or functionality beyond their authorized level.

A(Abuse)*

  • Abuse threats involve scenarios where legitimate system features are used in unintended ways that may lead to security vulnerabilities or breaches. This can include exploiting functionality to gain unauthorized access, bypass security controls, or manipulate data in harmful ways.

* Here Abuse term considered to be a add-on related to systenm features or vulnerable features can be used intentionally to breach the information

Practical Example

Taking an example of very common and generic architecture and differentiate the Assets, Controls and Threat Mapping into it.

  1. Assets: Assets refer to the valuable resources that an organization wants to protect. These can include physical assets such as buildings and equipment, as well as digital assets such as data, software, intellectual property, and customer information. Identifying assets is the first step in threat modeling because it helps organizations prioritize their security efforts based on what needs protection the most.
  1. Control: Controls are the security measures put in place to protect assets from threats. Controls can be administrative (e.g., policies and procedures), technical (e.g., firewalls and encryption), or physical (e.g., locks and security guards). Control identification involves assessing what security measures are currently in place and what additional controls may be needed to mitigate potential threats.
  1. Threats or Threats Mapping: Threats are potential events or circumstances that could harm an organization's assets. Threats can come from various sources, including malicious actors (e.g., hackers and insiders), natural disasters, accidents, and technological failures. Threat identification involves identifying the different types of threats that could affect an organization's assets and assessing their likelihood and potential impact.
